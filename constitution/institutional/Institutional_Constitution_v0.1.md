# Institutional Constitution v0.1

**Status**: Frozen (Ratified for v0.x exploratory use)  
**Date**: 2026-07-23  
**Scope**: Governs institutional legitimacy, values, authority, and amendment for any organization adopting this framework

---

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of this framework is to establish a durable constitutional foundation for the creation, governance, evolution, and preservation of knowledge. The framework exists to ensure that knowledge is acquired, evaluated, revised, and retired through transparent, reproducible, and evidence-governed processes, independent of any particular implementation, technology, or reasoning system.

It is intended for adoption by any number of independent institutions—research groups, open-source communities, laboratories, or individual practitioners—that wish to participate in a shared Epistemic Civilization while retaining organizational autonomy.

---

## 2. Constitutional Laws

**Law I — Governed Knowledge**  
Knowledge enters, evolves, and is retired only through governed epistemic processes.

**Law II — Replaceable Implementations**  
No implementation defines the institution. Models, platforms, agents, repositories, and storage systems are replaceable. The constitutional layer persists.

**Law III — Replayable Evolution**  
Every material change to institutional knowledge SHALL be attributable, reproducible, replayable, and permanently linked to its provenance.

---

## 3. Guiding Principles

1. **Uncertainty is preserved until evidence justifies reduction.**  
   Provisional objects, minority hypotheses, and explicit unknowns are first-class. Confidence is calibrated, not assumed.

2. **The institution exists to reduce uncertainty, not to maximize agreement.**  
   Contradiction is valuable. Falsification is rewarded. Consensus is never a substitute for evidence.

3. **Knowledge is governed; tools are replaceable.**  
   Epistemic rules and provenance take precedence over any particular AI system, notebook, agent framework, or infrastructure choice.

4. **Observation is open; commitment is gated.**  
   Actions that only observe or propose do not require ratification. Actions that commit the institution do.

5. **Recognized authority remains ultimate on constitutional and high-stakes epistemic matters.**  
   Certain categories of change require explicit ratification by a recognized authority before institutional state may transition.

---

## 4. Constitutional States of Knowledge

All epistemic objects recognized by an adopting Institution SHALL occupy exactly one Constitutional State at any point in time. Constitutional States govern institutional standing rather than internal computational representation. Detailed object lifecycles are defined by the Computational Constitution (CEK).

| State            | Meaning |
|------------------|--------|
| **Observed**     | Raw external information that has been detected but has not yet entered institutional knowledge. |
| **Provisional**  | Published through governed intake. Exists within the institution. Not endorsed. May be contradictory or low-confidence. |
| **Institutional**| Governed knowledge that has passed all required ratification gates. May influence decisions and further research. |
| **Historical**   | Retired, superseded, or archived. Never deleted. Remains part of the immutable record. |

---

## 5. Ratification Gates

A **Ratification Gate** is a constitutionally recognized decision point that requires explicit authorization from a recognized authority before institutional state may transition.

**Recognized Authority** — An entity constitutionally authorized by an adopting Institution to ratify Constitutional State transitions or institutional commitments. By default this authority is human. Additional authorities MAY be designated only through constitutional amendment.

### 5.1 Gate Categories

| Category                                      | Constitutional Requirement          |
|-----------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Constitutional amendment                      | MUST be ratified                    |
| Theory replacement or major revision          | MUST be ratified                    |
| Formal publication or external release        | MUST be ratified                    |
| High-impact Decision                          | MUST be ratified                    |
| Critical-path repository merge                | MAY be policy-defined               |
| Evidence ingestion / Provisional publication  | MUST NOT require ratification       |
| Question creation                             | MUST NOT require ratification       |
| Hypothesis proposal                           | MUST NOT require ratification       |
| Experiment design or scheduling               | MAY be policy-defined               |
| Promotion (Provisional → Institutional)       | MAY be policy-defined               |
| Archival or retirement (→ Historical)         | MAY be policy-defined               |

### 5.2 Normative Rules

- Where this Constitution states “MUST be ratified,” no automated process may complete the transition without recorded authorization from a recognized authority.
- Where this Constitution states “MAY be policy-defined,” the Research Controller or the relevant Research Program MUST publish an explicit rule. In the absence of a published rule, ratification is required.
- Every act of ratification is itself a Decision event and MUST be recorded with provenance, rationale, authority, and timestamp.
- Delegation of authority is permitted. The act of delegation MUST itself be recorded as a Decision.

Detailed procedures, notification mechanisms, timeouts, and escalation paths belong in subordinate specifications and policies, not in this Constitution.

---

## 6. Governance and Amendment

- This Constitution may be amended only by explicit, recorded decision of the adopting Institution’s designated recognized authorities.
- Proposed amendments MUST state the rationale, the affected sections, and the expected consequences.
- Amendments are versioned. Prior versions remain part of the immutable historical record.
- Changes to this Constitution require identification of a concrete ambiguity, inconsistency, or governance failure. New ideas alone are not sufficient grounds for amendment.
- The Computational Constitution (CEK), Epistemic Intake Specification (EIS), Research Controller Specification (RC), and Research Program Specification (RPS) are subordinate technical standards. They MAY evolve under their own versioning rules provided they remain consistent with this Institutional Constitution.

---

## 7. Relationship to Subordinate Specifications

This Constitution is the sole source of institutional legitimacy. The following specifications are authorized by it and MUST remain consistent with its Laws and Principles:

1. **Computational Constitution (CEK)** — Defines epistemic objects, legal operations, invariants, constitutional event vocabulary, and capability metrics.
2. **Epistemic Intake Specification (EIS)** — Defines how raw inputs become governed epistemic objects, including Provisional publication.
3. **Research Controller Specification (RC)** — Defines event processing, routing, enforcement of Ratification Gates, and dispatch.
4. **Research Program Specification (RPS)** — Defines the structure, lifecycle, and resource claims of long-lived research initiatives.

No implementation or tool may claim constitutional authority.

## 8. Versioning

- This document is versioned as Institutional Constitution vX.Y.
- v0.x denotes exploratory status; structural change is permitted.
- v1.0 denotes a ratified, stable constitution. Subsequent changes follow the amendment process in Section 6.
